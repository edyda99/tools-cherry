How daily calories are worked out

The calculator first estimates your basal metabolic rate (BMR) — the calories your body burns at rest — using the Mifflin–St Jeor equation:

  • Men: 10 × weight(kg) + 6.25 × height(cm) − 5 × age + 5
  • Women: 10 × weight(kg) + 6.25 × height(cm) − 5 × age − 161

It then multiplies your BMR by an activity factor (1.2 for sedentary up to 1.9 for extra active) to get your Total Daily Energy Expenditure (TDEE) — the calories needed to maintain your current weight.

To lose or gain weight, you adjust intake from there. Since roughly 7,700 calories equals one kilogram (about 3,500 per pound) of body fat, a 500-calorie daily deficit works out to about half a kilogram, or one pound, per week.

Common calorie questions

How many calories do I need a day? It depends on your age, sex, height, weight, and activity. Enter them above and the calculator shows your maintenance number.

What is TDEE? Total Daily Energy Expenditure — all the calories you burn in a day. Eat around it to stay the same weight, less to lose, more to gain.

How many calories to lose weight? About 500 fewer than your TDEE each day gives roughly half a kilogram (one pound) of loss per week. The table above shows gentler and faster options too.

Which formula does this use? The Mifflin–St Jeor equation, which dietitians widely recommend for healthy adults.

Is it safe to eat very few calories? Very low intakes can be unsafe. Many guidelines advise staying above about 1,200 calories a day for women and 1,500 for men without medical supervision.

This calculator is for general information only and is not medical or nutritional advice. Talk to your doctor or a registered dietitian before making big changes to how you eat.
