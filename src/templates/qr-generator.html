Static vs dynamic QR codes — and why it matters

There are two kinds of QR code, and the difference decides whether yours keeps working. A static code, like the ones here, has the actual destination baked into the black-and-white pattern. Scan it and the phone reads the URL, WiFi details, or contact card directly. Nothing sits between the code and its data, so it works forever and offline — the only way it breaks is if the page it points to is taken down.

A dynamic code stores a short link that bounces through a third-party service before redirecting to the real destination. That lets the provider edit the target later or track scans, but it ties your code to their account. If you stop paying, or they shut the service down, every printed code stops working — which is why people are caught out when a "free" QR service starts charging or expires their codes. For anything you print on packaging, business cards, posters, or signage, a static code is the safer choice because it can't be switched off.

Why generating in your browser (and no login) matters

This tool runs the QR library on the page itself, so the data you type — a WiFi password, a contact's phone number, a private link — is never sent to a server. With sign-up generators, that same information passes through someone else's system and is often logged. No-login also means no account to manage, no email harvested, no usage cap, and no watermark stamped on your code. You get the finished image immediately, as a crisp PNG for documents and printing or an SVG that scales to any poster size without blurring.

One practical tip for reliable scanning: always leave the white border (the "quiet zone") around the code, keep good contrast between the foreground and background colors, and print it large enough — a dense code with a long URL or High error correction needs more room to stay readable.

Common QR code questions

Do I need an account or login? No. There's no login, signup, account, or email required — make as many codes as you like, free, with no limits and no watermark. Everything is generated on your device, so there's nothing to register for.

Do these codes expire? No. They are static, so the data lives in the pattern itself — there is nothing to renew or switch off, and they never expire.

PNG or SVG — which should I pick? PNG for documents, slides, and most printing; SVG when you need a poster-size code or are handing it to a designer.

What does error correction do? It lets the code still scan when part of it is scuffed or covered. Medium is a sensible default; use High for small or hard-wearing prints.

Why won't my code scan? Usually it's too small, too dense, or has no white border. Print it larger, shorten the URL, or leave a clear margin around it.
